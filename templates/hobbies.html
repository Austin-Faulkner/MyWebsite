My hobbies and interests for the past 15 years have been mostly in mathematics and physics. But about 6 years ago (2016) I started thinking
deeply about computational problems. The promises of machine learning, quantum computing, and solutions programming started to fill my mind.
As such I started thinking seriously about becoming a software engineer in an interesting field which would allow for daily stimulation and challenges.
It's vital to not only love what you do but also to be challenged intellectually by what you do. A degree in computer science offers
a window into this very realistic possibility.

I love to read and learn. Critical thinking is an absolute pleasure. I enjoy working through proofs and thinking deeply about seemingly trivial
matters such as the Triangle Inequality, the square root of 2, Euler's identity, and things like what is "mass" really (?!?). I also really enjoy the deep stuff like
complex analysis, real analysis, vector calculus, symmetries, and what have you. Mathematics takes an immense amount of time to learn and to become proficient in. So does
software engineering (SE), except in the case of SE one learns islands of expertise much more quickly. For example, should one choose, one can pick up the
syntax of a programming langauge in one day. This doesn't mean one is then proficient in the language; but, it does mean that one now has the tools required
to begin creating beautiful applications. And what's especially fun about making applications is, unlike mathematical proofs, one can see the application's results
"instantaneously". Watch it compile--or fail--and one immediately learns something new. That feeling is uniquely gratifying. What's also great about SE is the team work
inherent to the discipline. I really enjoy working with and learning from others. Iron sharpens iron. And I want to partner with those who like me are builders, do-ers, and makers!

Last but certainly not least, I like hiking and camping with my wife and pit/basset-hound mix named Hurley. I love to laugh, and I love my family.

    Some YouTube videos of some of the research I conducted in an ML course at Texas State University

    This video captures Guadalupe Street, right next to The University of Texas at Austin. Here, I've applied a neueral network architecture (a convolutional neural
    network, or CNN) to analyze the semantic segmentation (also called Panoptic Segmentation) you see in the computer's line of sight. The video analysis also embeds
    an Instance Segmentation wherein the CNN is able to identify cars, trucks, people, and traffic lights with a real-time probability estimation. Basically, the computer
    vision techniques are attempting to identify anything an automated vehicle would want to be crucially aware of while cruising down the road.




    In this video, the CNN is applying the same techniques, but, in addition, I've applied Keypoint Detection (KD). The Keypoint Detection is hardly noticeable
    in the video given there are few subjects in the computer's line of sight to which to apply KD. But if you look closely to the billboard and the people walking,
    you'll find a hint of what KD is up to. I'll post another video which strictly showcases KD and it's unique analytic benefits down the line. To summarize though,
    the benefit of KD is the CV ability to detect animated behavior. So, for instance, if someone is crossing the street at a certain speed, the CV picks up on
    the angular movements of the individual and can potentially calculate the individual's velocity, which is important if the automated vehicle is to know the rate at
    which the vehicle should come to a stop. The titles of the next three videos will indicate which isolated methods are used in the experiment. What's nice about
    the three remaining videos is they isolate the techniques demonstrating each's usefulness. (More to come.)
