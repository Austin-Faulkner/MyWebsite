About Me


















I started coding not long ago--about 2.5 years ago. I became interested in coding during my Vanderbilt University physics internship when we coded in Fortran. The attraction I suppose is that it was tidy and, once understood, it all made logical sense. I've been interested in logic and the roles it plays in everything from everyday reasoning, to mathematical proofs, to reasoning through a physics problem (itself built on math and physics intuition), to discovering the foundation of computability itself.

My avatar in the navbar is of Kurt Gödel in the days when he would walk around the Institute of Advanced Study at Princeton with the great Einstein. Jim Holt writes in his, "When Einstein Walked with Gödel," that Einstein is known for having said he comes to work just so he can talk to Gödel. And so they did. They walked and talked. (See picture).




Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems paper in 1931 set the stage for what would later be Turing's Halting problem. I recommend this book "Computability and Logic" for more information. It's fascinating to say the least that lonesome theorems in logic would have something "say" about both the structure of mathematics and computing. Gödel "passes the torch" to Alan Turing, and from Turing's work we find the inception of today's information processing devices.

So my introduction to computer science is a bit windy to say the least. It began in theory of discourse update semantics in computational linguistics on which I wrote a lengthy capstone paper in summer 2008 for the Abilene Christian University honors department (paper 1, paper 2). From there I discovered myself on a journey into mathematics and logic itself, a journey which continues today. Futher roads led to the very practical world of computational physics research at Vanderbilt University where I was on a theory team for several months. There, we studied the physical properties of graphene nanotubule electron transport phenomena. At the time, my physics education was nascent at best--I had only had Engineering Physics I. So my understanding of how to ssh into the parallel computer network in the theoretical physics lab and calculate eigenvalues for the ground-state of an electron using Fortran was sketchy at best. But I learned how to work hard "in the dark", so to speak, and the challenge was definitely rewarding. The power of computational methods to probe the quantum astounded me. I had to know more about it all.

On my resume, you find will a lot about my intellectual journey. You'll have also find the usual suspects--for instance, work history, education, accolades, etc. Nevertheless, I've also provided an abbreviated version of each of these facets of my curiosity in the navbar tabs above.

In the meantime, I've spent the last 3-4 years wresting my journey down from the proverbial aether into a much more satisfyingly terrestrial and practical domain of interests. And, wow, can I say I'm loving it! I simply cannot wait to work as a software engineer.
